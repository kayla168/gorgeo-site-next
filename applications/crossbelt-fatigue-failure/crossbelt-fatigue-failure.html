A customer’s bolts sheared off—again. But this time, the torque was right, the material was fine, and the inspection passed. This teardown explains why the flange face—not the bolt—was the hidden culprit, and how we fixed it through force-path redesign.

The Crossbelt Module: Good Torque, Still Broken

  • Bolts? M8, Grade 10.9
  • Torque? 25 Nm, fully verified
  • Anti-corrosion? Fluorocarbon coating applied
Three fatigue fractures, one module, zero warning. Snap. Snap. Snap.

The assumption? “Maybe the bolts were weak.” But the bolts were fine. The problem was the preload — and how the flange was pretending to be flat.

False Assumptions: Why Flat Isn’t Always Flat

The original design looked standard: flat flange, adequate bolt depth, threadlocker applied.

But two critical things were missed:

  • Flange surface was tilted ~2.8°
  • Coating thickness was uneven

This led to local contact only, not full-face engagement. Result?

  • Torque decay from eccentric preload
  • Stress riser at first thread
  • Accelerated fatigue fracture

Root Cause: A Classic Misjudgment

The core issue was a classic misjudgment in mating faces, leading to a cascade of failures.

We didn’t need a stronger bolt. We needed to change how the structure carried force.

❶ Rebuild the Contact Face

  • Traced real load ring with imprint testing
  • Redesigned flange with R0.3 concavity + 2° correction

❷ Redirect the Force Path

  • Added a preload-guiding washer with staged shoulder
  • Ensured axial closure, removed lateral stress paths

❸ Validate the Fix Like a System

  • CMM inspection on contact surface (not just thread)
  • Torque-angle curve analysis to detect early slip

Fixing Fatigue With Smarter Geometry

The solution was not about torque, but about creating a stable geometric foundation for the preload.

  • Preload relies on stable contact closure.
  • Contact relies on the actual bearing face, not just assumed flatness.

Final Thoughts & Next Steps

This wasn’t a bolt failure. It was a force path failure hiding behind a “flat-looking” flange. Fatigue fracture is often the final, catastrophic symptom of chronic vibration and preload loss.
